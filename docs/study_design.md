# Study Design

## Project Overview

This project investigates how extreme temperatures and PM2.5 air pollution vary across U.S. cities and how these factors may contribute to environmental health risk.

The project uses weather and air quality data with statistical modeling methods to identify relationships between heat, pollution, and environmental risk.

---

## Research Question

How can statistical models quantify and predict environmental health risk by analyzing the relationship between extreme temperatures and PM2.5 concentrations across U.S. cities?

---

## Objectives

- Collect temperature and air quality data from U.S. cities.
- Analyze relationships between temperature and PM2.5 concentrations.
- Identify patterns in environmental risk.
- Build statistical models to quantify relationships.
- Create a dashboard to visualize results.

---

## Study Locations

| City | Reason |
|---|---|
| Phoenix, AZ | Extreme desert heat |
| Sacramento, CA | Heat and wildfire smoke |
| Los Angeles, CA | Urban pollution and heat |
| Seattle, WA | Cooler climate comparison |
| Houston, TX | Heat and humidity |

---

## Study Period

Data will be collected from:

May 1, 2022 - September 30, 2025

This provides multiple seasons and summer heat periods for analysis.

---

## Data Sources

### Weather Data
Source: Open-Meteo API

Variables:
- Maximum temperature
- Average temperature
- Humidity
- Wind speed
- Precipitation

---

## Hypotheses

1. Higher temperatures may be associated with increased environmental risk.

2. Higher PM2.5 concentrations may be associated with increased environmental risk.

3. Environmental risk patterns will differ between cities with different climates.

---

## Planned Analysis

The project will use:

- Data visualization
- Correlation analysis
- Regression modeling
- Environmental risk scoring
